Coins

The concepts of "sterling" and "pound sterling" came into use in England in the Middle Ages.
240 small coins of a certain weight were minted from 1 pound of silver – pennies
(or, as such coins were also called, sterling), which until the XIII century were the most common money in
England. In fact, the very concept of "pound sterling" was originally only a counting monetary
unit and literally meant the weight measure "pound" in silver coins.

Over time, the denominations became more and more diverse. Shillings appeared, each of
which was equal to 12 pennies or 48 farthings. The pound, respectively, was equal to 20
shillings. Shillings, nicknamed "Bob" in England, were minted en masse until 1966 inclusive.
The exception was 750,000 coins with the date "1970", prepared for the "farewell" set of old-
-style coins.

Pennies (or pennies) are still minted in the UK

A farthing is a small change coin, literally translated as a quarter. For the first time such coins
in the ¼ penny in England appeared in the XIII century. Up to and including the XVI century
and for some time in the XIX century, farthings were minted from silver, the rest of the time –
from copper and bronze. The minting of coins of this denomination was discontinued in 1956.

For the first time, a large coin equal to 240 pennies or 20 shillings was minted in the XV
century during the reign of Henry the Seventh and was called a sovereign. The coinage of such
a coin was made of gold.
In the XVI century, under the English Queen Elizabeth the First, a coin of 20 silver shillings
began to be called a "pound" (in English, "pound"), and coins of 30 shillings, i.e. one and a half
pounds sterling, began to be called a sovereign.
The next king of England after Elizabeth, James the First, briefly returned the 20-shilling coin
to its former name, but during his reign this coin changed its name twice more: first to united, then to laurel.
Under Charles the Second, in 1663, the guinea became the main coin of Britain – a gold coin
equated to 21 shillings, and thus amounting to 1.05 pounds sterling.

In 1694, the first paper banknotes were issued in Britain. The denominations of the
banknotes were indicated in pounds sterling and were large – from 20 to 1000. Banknotes of
smaller denominations began to appear only in the second half of the XVIII century – 10
pounds in 1759, 5 pounds in 1793, banknotes of 1 and 2 pounds in 1797.
At the beginning of the XIX century, when a single gold standard was established in Great
Britain, the sovereign, equated in value to the pound sterling, became the main major coin again.

The coinage of the sovereign as a coin in circulation continued until the eighties of the XX
century. Nowadays, the sovereign is issued only as collectible and investment coins.
The crown is originally a gold coin. Its release was started in the XVI century under King Henry
the Eighth. In the first year of its appearance, it was initially equal to 4 shillings and 6 pennies,
then 5 shillings. In different periods, the crown was minted from gold and silver. Starting from
the reign of George the Sixth, crowns began to be minted from a copper-nickel alloy.

Banknotes

In 1971, the United Kingdom made the transition to the decimal system, as a result of which
the pound was equated to 100 pence. The following year, 1972, the British government gave
the British pound the opportunity to be valued using a "floating rate", which was not
supported by the Bank of England. Thus, the exchange rate of the national currency was
determined on the basis of trading on the international currency market. The result of this in
1977 was a record low exchange rate of the main monetary unit of Great Britain – $ 1.72 per
pound sterling. For comparison: back in the 40s, they gave more than $ 4 per pound, and in
the 60s up to 2.8. Today, however, the cost difference in the national currencies of the UK and
the USA is even smaller: in 2020, they give about $ 1.3 per pound.
To date, banknotes of 5, 10, 20 and 50 pounds are in circulation in the UK. Now in this country
they issue banknotes of the "G" series - this line of bills has been in circulation since 2007.

In addition to the money issued by Great Britain, there are coins and banknotes that are issued by overseas territories - autonomies that are under the sovereignty of the United
Kingdom, but are not part of it. There are currently 14 such territories in the UK. Coins of some
of them are also in circulation in the British Isles. Also, banknotes denominated in pounds
sterling are issued by three banks of Scotland (have full circulation only there), the Bank of
England (have circulation in England and Wales) and four Northern Irish banks (are settlement
marks of Northern Ireland). These regional issues, although they are not full-fledged money,
but represent something like a bill of exchange, can be accepted throughout the United
Kingdom.
